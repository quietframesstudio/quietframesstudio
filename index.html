Shooting
film
slowly.

I'm a film photographer based just outside of Boston, working across 35mm, medium format, and 4×5. Most of my work is made within an hour or two of home — city streets, coastlines, parks, and the quiet in-between spaces that don't announce themselves.

I shoot film because it's a choice, not a nostalgia trip. In a world where a single memory card can hold thousands of images, I'd rather load a roll of 36 and stop there. That constraint isn't a limitation — it's the whole point. When you know you have 36 frames, or 12, or 8, you slow down. You look longer before you lift the camera. You think about the light, the moment, whether this is actually the frame. That deliberateness changes what you see and what you make.

The same is true of the tools. An old camera with a prime lens doesn't autofocus, doesn't bracket exposures, doesn't do the thinking for you. Every decision is yours — focus, aperture, timing. That friction is what I'm after.

And then there's what comes after the shoot. I develop my own black and white and color film at home. I watch the images emerge from the tank. I cut the negatives, scan them, process them. The whole sequence takes time, and it can't be rushed. That's not inconvenience — that's the ritual. There's something meditative about a process that demands your full attention and gives you no shortcuts. At the end of it, I have something physical: a strip of film I can hold up to the light, that exists in the world independent of any hard drive or cloud account. I own it. That matters to me.

My work tends to live in quiet moments — subtle light, everyday interactions, the kind of intimacy that exists in public spaces without announcing itself. A lot of it is also tied to fatherhood and memory, photographing not just what things look like, but what they feel like to live through. Even when my family isn't in the frame, they're often part of the moment.

I'm less interested in perfect images and more interested in honest ones.

Prints are available. If you'd like to discuss a purchase or commission, I'd love to hear from you.
